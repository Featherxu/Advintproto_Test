Programming Design Systems

1.

"We are now in transition from an object-oriented to a systems-oriented culture. Here change emanates, not from things, but from the way things are done."

This first sentence immediately triggers me as a designer who just learned about object-oriented programming. But that make a lot of sense to me because in this world, everything is closely tied with something else, together made up a system, to have a 1+1>2 effect. That's why as a designer, it's our responsibility to think bigger, to think about the system instead of thinking some little pieces (features) first.

2.

"Meta-design is much more difficult than design; it is easier to draw something than to explain how to draw it. One of the problems is that different sets of potential specifications cannot easily be envisioned all at once. Another is that a computer has to be told absolutely everything. However, once we have successfully explained how to draw something in a sufficiently general manner, the same explanation will work for related shapes, in different circumstances; so the time spent in formulating a precise explanation turns out to be worth it."

The front part saying "it is easier to draw something than to explain how to draw it"really makes me feel relatable, because when we are trying to code something, we might know how to do it in Figma or photoshop, but when we are trying to code it out, we need to think about the logic behind like how to make the system make sense or organize it. We are trying to communicate our vision and our ideas.

3.

"One of the most fundamental skills in graphic design: The ability to create visual relationships that make sense for your content."

Designing on paper or Figma is very different with coding it. Doing on paper you can design where the point is going to be, but on a software, we have different constraint that the points around is preset and it's hard to change it. Chapter 2 "The Internet of Things"

1.

"Like the smartphone, the internet of things isn’t a single technology, but an unruly assemblage of protocols,  sensing regimes, capabilities and desires, all swept under a single rubric for the sake of disciplinary convenience."

Before I read this article, when mentions the IOT, all I think about is "wow this is so cool, seems like high technology". But never thought about the protocols behind it. I thought it's an adds on to our current devices, but it's actually another more complex system that needs to run more data behind, and problems can happens even more than usual mobile does.

2.

"If wearable biometric devices are aimed, however imperfectly, at rigorous self-mastery, the colonization of the domestic environment by similarly networked products and services is intended to deliver a very different experience: convenience. The clear aim of such “smart home” efforts is to as nearly as possible short-circuit the process of reflection that stands between one’s recognition of a desire and its fulfillment via the market."

The smart devices who aims to bring "convenience" to the users, the end goal is just to let them purchase in a quicker and non-thinking way. When things are getting easier, less thinking process is included which is also very crucial to people. The benefit at the end still lands at the business side but many people are not aware of it.

3.

"You get your detergent on time, yes, but Amazon gets so much more. They get data on the time and place of your need, as well as its frequency and intensity, and that data has value. It is, explicitly, an asset, and you can be sure they will exploit that asset in every way their terms and conditions permit them to— including by using it to develop behavioral models that map the terrain of our desires, so as to exploit them with even greater efficiency in the future."

I really like the example that the author gave about Amazon button. It's a button that when you click it, it knows ur running out of grocery and it can but it for u automatically and immediately. It's true that it beings a lot of values to the users. But at the backend, the data Amazon can get and make use of is enormous. By knowing what kind of grocery you need at what exact time and exact frequency, and knowing side data like your age, your family situation, your gender, the big data and analysis so many information while there's no protocol to regulate these similar products. It triggers me because I never thought about that side of the fact before.
